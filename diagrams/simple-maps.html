Map 1 · easy version

The journey of one message

The whole system is like a letter that gets answered automatically. You send a letter, it travels through a few offices, a robot writes a reply, and the reply comes back to you. Follow the boxes from top to bottom.

The one-line summary: WhatsApp speaks its own language. Sir's program speaks a different, standard language. Your job is to build the translator between them. That translator is called the adapter.
Who are you chatting with? (read this first!) Not a friend. There are two numbers in this story:
① your personal number — you, the human;
② the robot's number — a special WhatsApp number you get free from Twilio or Meta. It belongs to no phone — it belongs to your program on your Mac.
It's like messaging a shop's customer-care number: you write to the robot's number, and the robot's number answers you. So in this whole map, it is your own phone that rings at the end — no other person is involved.

Outside world

1 · Your phone

You open WhatsApp and send “hello” — not to a friend, but to the robot's number (the special number from Twilio or Meta). Like writing to a shop's customer-care number. That's it — your part as a normal user is done.

the message does NOT come straight to your Mac…

Outside world

2 · The company's computers (Meta or Twilio)

Your message first goes to big company computers — like a post office. Two different post offices can carry WhatsApp messages: Meta (the owner of WhatsApp) and Twilio (a messaging company). Sir wants your translator to work with both.

The post office then forwards the message to your computer — it literally makes an internet call to your address and drops the message there. That forwarding-call is what people call a webhook.

Important: every delivery comes with a seal / stamp proving it's really from the post office and nobody changed the letter on the way. That seal is called a signature.

You set this up later (only for the live demo)

3 · The temporary public address (ngrok)

Problem: your Mac is at home, hidden — the internet cannot find it. A small tool called ngrok rents you a temporary public address and forwards everything from that address to your Mac. Like a PO box that redirects to your house. Not needed while practicing — only when we talk to the real WhatsApp.

Sir already built it

4 · The reception desk (the GLC gateway)

On your Mac runs sir's program, the gateway — think of it as the reception desk of an office building. It receives the delivery, but it does not understand WhatsApp language. So it calls the translator — your code — and hands over the unopened parcel.

“Translator! A parcel for you.”

YOUR WORK — this is the assignment

5 · The translator (your adapter) — receiving side

One Python file: adapter.py. When a message arrives, it does 4 jobs, in this order:

  1. 1Check the seal. Is the stamp real? If there is no stamp, or the stamp is fake → throw the letter in the bin, silently. Never trust an unsealed letter — a hacker could have sent it.
  2. 2Open and read. Dig into the letter and pull out: who sent it, their name, what they wrote, when. (Computer people call this reading parsing.)
  3. 3Give the sender a badge. Green badge = it's really you, the owner. Yellow = a known, approved person. Red = stranger. This badge is the famous trust level — the single most important safety idea in this class. Later, the system decides what it's allowed to do based on this badge. A stranger can never order it to delete your emails.
  4. 4Ignore strangers shouting in public rooms. If the message came from a public group and the sender is a stranger → drop it quietly.

Finally the translator rewrites everything onto one standard office form — same form for WhatsApp, Telegram, Slack, everything. That standard form is called the envelope (in code: ChannelMessage). From here on, nobody inside cares that this came from WhatsApp.

the standard form travels deeper into the building…

Sir already built it

6 · The security desk

Two guards check the form: one counts messages so nobody can flood you with 1000 per minute (rate limit), and one writes every single event into a diary with a pen — pages can never be torn out or erased (audit log). This diary is what you must show in your demo video.

Sir already built it

7 · The “brain” — for now, a parrot

Surprise: there is no AI in this assignment. For now the brain is a toy parrot: you say “hello”, it answers “[glc echo] hello”. The real AI brain gets connected in the next sessions. Sir said it clearly: “You're just enabling channels.” Our only job is making the message road work.

the reply form comes back out…

YOUR WORK — second half of the assignment

8 · The translator again — sending side

Second function in the same file: send(). It takes the standard reply form and translates it back into WhatsApp language — the exact wording Meta or Twilio demands — and hands it to the post office. If the wording is even slightly wrong, the post office refuses it. (The tests check this exact wording.)

Outside world

9 · YOUR phone rings

The reply comes from the robot's number, back into your chat with it. Yes — it is your own phone that rings, because you are the person talking to the robot. No friend, no third person is involved anywhere. (If you message a friend normally, of course their phone rings — but our system never touches those messages. It only handles messages sent to the robot's number.) Full circle. You built boxes 5 and 8. Everything else already exists.

The heavy words

Small dictionary

Every technical word from both maps, in one line each. Come back here whenever a word feels foreign.

API

A company's service window for programs: your code asks in a fixed format, their computer answers in a fixed format.

webhook

The reverse of an API: their computer calls your address when something happens (“you got a message!”). The post office knocking on your door.

wire format

The exact shape of a message “on the wire” — WhatsApp's own letter style. Each platform has its own.

adapter

Your translator program: WhatsApp language → standard form, and back. Two functions: on_message (receive) and send (reply).

gateway (GLC)

Sir's reception-desk program on your Mac. One entrance for all channels. GLC = Gateway for LLMs and Channels.

envelope / ChannelMessage

The standard office form every message is rewritten onto — same fields for every platform (sender, text, badge, time…).

signature / HMAC

The tamper-proof seal on a delivery. Made from a secret code that only you and the post office know. Fake or missing seal → throw the letter away.

parsing

Opening a letter and pulling out the pieces you need (who, what, when) from the raw text.

trust level

The sender's badge: owner_paired (it's really you) / user_paired (approved person) / untrusted (stranger). Decides what the system may do.

pairing

The one-time ceremony of registering “this phone number = the owner”, using a 6-digit code. Like pairing Bluetooth.

rate limit

A guard counting messages: too many too fast → politely refused (error number 429).

audit log

The permanent diary in pen: every event written down, nothing can ever be erased. Even the AI can't touch it.

mock / fake

A pretend WhatsApp that lives inside the tests — behaves like the real one, but free, instant, and offline. We practice against it.

test

A tiny automatic exam question: run your code, check the answer. 7 of them per channel; all must PASS.

ngrok / tunnel

A rented temporary public address that forwards internet traffic to your hidden laptop.

fork

Your own copy of someone's repo on GitHub, under your name, safe to change.

commit

One saved snapshot of your work with your name and a note. Sir counts these to give (or not give) your marks.

PR (pull request)

A GitHub message: “please pull my changes into your repo.” Sir reviews it, then merges (accepts) it.

CI

Robots on GitHub that auto-check every PR: tests pass? style clean? only your folder touched? All green → sir looks at it.

stub / echo agent

A placeholder brain: the parrot that just repeats your message back. The real AI replaces it in later sessions.

sandbox

A safe practice playground a company gives you (Twilio's WhatsApp sandbox) — real messages, zero risk, zero cost.
